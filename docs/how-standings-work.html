How Standings Work

Who's
Winning?

Every Rumble format ranks players a little differently. Here's how each one decides the standings — first in plain English, then a deep-dive into the math for the curious.

Plain English Round Robin Gauntlet Stack King of the Court Finals Under the Hood

01The plain-English version

No math required. Find your format and read the card.

The big idea (Round Robin & Gauntlet): you're ranked by how convincingly you win, not just by how many points you pile up. Winning a tight 13–11 battle counts the same as an 11–9 win — grinding out extra points doesn't help. Winning big does help, but with diminishing returns, so there's no reward for running up the score. And it's adjusted for who you played with and against.
Round Robin

Adjusted Margin

Everyone plays a set number of rounds, with partners and opponents rotating so you mix with the whole group. Mixed mode constrains every team to 1 from each of two groups — whether that's Men / Women for mixed doubles, Advanced / Social for skill balancing, or your own custom labels.

  • Margin matters. How much you win (or lose) by drives your rank — with diminishing returns, so a blowout beats a squeaker but not by a runaway amount.
  • Deuce points don't inflate. A 13–11 win counts exactly like an 11–9 win. Both are "won by 2."
  • Strength of schedule. Beating strong teams counts more; being carried by a strong partner counts less.
  • Byes & unfinished games never count.
Ranked by Adjusted Margin → ties broken by win rate → head-to-head → a fixed random draw.
Gauntlet

Climb the Courts

Eight players. After every round, everyone is re-ranked by the same Adjusted Margin as Round Robin, then re-paired by rank.

  • Court 1 hosts the top four: #1 + #4 vs #2 + #3. (Mixed mode picks the 1M+1W split within each block.)
  • Court 2 hosts the next four: #5 + #8 vs #6 + #7.
  • Play well and you climb toward Court 1; slip and you slide down.
Same engine as Round Robin — final standings use Adjusted Margin.
Stack · Ladder

Higher Courts Pay More

Courts are ranked, with Court 1 at the top. Where you win matters as much as that you win.

  • Points on higher courts are worth more — up to 1.5× on the top court, scaling down to the bottom.
  • Winning earns a bonus, and winning below the top court earns an extra climb bonus.
  • Keep winning to climb the ladder toward Court 1.
Ranked by average Stack score per game → ties broken by a fixed random draw.
King of the Court

Hold the Throne

Two courts. Court 1 is the King's Court — the throne. Win there to reign; lose and you drop.

  • Your score rewards wins, points scored, and especially wins on the King's Court.
  • The more time you spend winning at the top, the higher you finish.
Ranked by average King score per game → ties broken by a fixed random draw.
Finals

Brackets of Four

After the regular rounds, players are seeded straight from the standings into brackets of four:

  • Top 4 → Championship (1 vs 4, 2 vs 3).
  • Next groups → Consolation, Bronze, … one bracket per available court.
  • Players left over below the last full group of four are listed as unseated.
Seeding uses your regular-season rank. Finals results never change the regular standings.

Why your standing keeps moving — even for games you already played

This is the part that surprises people most, so it's worth a moment. In Round Robin and Gauntlet, the standings get more accurate as the session goes on — which means rounds you've already finished can be re-valued. Here's the life of a result:

Step 1 · Early on

Everyone starts equal

At the start, the app has no results to go on, so it can't tell who's strong yet. Every opponent and partner is treated the same, and your winning margins count at face value.

Step 2 · As you play

It learns who's strong

Round by round, results pile up and a picture forms of how strong each player is — based on how convincingly everyone has been winning and losing across the whole group.

Step 3 · Looking back

Earlier games get re-weighted

The app then revisits your past games with what it now knows. A win over someone who turned out to be a top player counts for more; a win over someone who's been struggling counts for a little less.

A quick example. In round 1 you beat Dana 11–6, back when nobody knew much about anyone. By round 5, Dana has won nearly every other game — clearly one of the strongest players in the room. The app now counts your round-1 win more generously, so your standing can tick up between rounds — even in a round where you sat out for a bye. You didn't gain or lose anything; the picture just came into focus.
It settles — it doesn't swing wildly. Strength can only nudge a game's value within set limits, and winning always helps while losing always hurts — no amount of re-weighting can turn a win into something worth less than a loss. So the standings refine and settle as more becomes known; they don't lurch around at random. (Stack and King of the Court don't do this — they're scored purely on what happened on court.)

The fine print — still in plain English

A few more rules quietly shape every standing. Here they are, so nothing's left unsaid.

Per game, not total

It's an average

You're judged on your performance per game, not your running total. Playing more games never inflates your rank, and missing one never deflates it — so byes, late arrivals, and uneven game counts all stay fair. (This is true in every format.)

Byes

Sitting out is free

A bye simply doesn't count. It isn't a win and isn't a loss — it has no effect on your standing whatsoever.

Unfinished games

Only finished games count

Because pickleball is win-by-2, a tied score like 11–11 means the game isn't over. Unfinished games stay out of the standings until they're decided, so a stalled game can never pad anyone's numbers.

Breaking ties

If two players are even

Ties break differently by format (see each card above). In Round Robin and Gauntlet it's win rate first, then the head-to-head result if those two played each other. Every format ends with a fixed random draw set once at the start — so a tiebreak never shifts mid-event.

Haven't played yet

No games, no rank

Anyone who hasn't finished a game yet sits at the bottom until they do — there's simply nothing to measure them on.

02Under the hood

For the curious — exactly how each number is computed.

Adjusted Margin — Round Robin & Gauntlet

A player's ranking number is the average adjusted-margin credit across their decided games, built in three layers.

Layer 1  Diminishing-returns margin (per game). Take the team's point margin d = teamScore − oppScore (negative for a loss) and put it on a square-root scale:

ms = sign(d) · √|d|

Because pickleball is win-by-2, every decided game has |d| ≥ 2, so a win is always ≥ √2 ≈ +1.41 and a loss ≤ −1.41.

GameRaw marginMargin score (ms)
11–9 / 13–11 / any deuce win+2+1.41
11–6+5+2.24
11–3+8+2.83
11–0+11+3.32

A shutout is worth only ~2.4× a squeaker — not 5.5× as it would be under raw points. This is what removes the reward for grinding out extra points.

Layer 2  Base strength (first pass). Each player's base score is the average of their ms across decided games. Center it on the field:

sp = basep − fieldAverage

sp > 0 means stronger than the field, < 0 weaker. Early in a session everyone sits near 0, so the adjustment below starts small and grows as evidence accumulates.

Layer 3  Strength-adjusted credit (second pass). Re-walk each decided game from player p's view, with partner q and opponents o₁, o₂:

O = (so₁ + so₂) / 2 // opposing-team strength P = sq // partner strength adj = clamp( k · (O − P), −C, +C ) credit = ms + adj

The term is additive (performance-vs-expectation), so it behaves correctly whether you win or lose:

SituationEffect on credit
Beat a strong teamboosted (you overperformed)
Lost to a strong teampenalty softened (expected to lose)
Lost to a weak teampenalty increased
Carried by a strong partnerdiscounted
Carried a weak partnerboosted

Your final Adjusted Margin is the mean of credit over your decided games.

ConstantDefaultMeaning
curve√ (square root)diminishing-returns shape
k0.5strength sensitivity (opponent & partner)
C1.0max strength adjustment per game
The invariant: because C = 1.0 < √2 ≤ |ms|, the sign of credit always matches the sign of ms. Every win credits positive, every loss negative — strength weighting can reorder similar results, but a win is never worth less than a loss of the same margin.

Why a single pass? Base scores are computed once and used as fixed inputs for the adjustment — we deliberately do not iterate to a converged rating. On a short social session that would overfit and become impossible to explain. The light, fixed-strength heuristic stays predictable and transparent.

Worked example — why 3–0 still beats 2–1

Ignoring strength adjustment for clarity:

PlayerRecordGamesAvg margin score
A2–111–0, 11–0, 0–11(3.32 + 3.32 − 3.32) / 3 = 1.11
B3–011–9, 11–9, 11–9(1.41 × 3) / 3 = 1.41

Diminishing returns caps the value of those blowouts, so the unbeaten player (B) finishes ahead — winning more games reasserts itself, while margin still decides things between similar records.

Tiebreaker chain (Round Robin & Gauntlet)

  1. Average adjusted credit (Adjusted Margin) — primary
  2. Win rate (wins ÷ games)
  3. Head-to-head point differential in games the tied players faced each other (regular rounds only)
  4. Fixed random draw assigned once at tournament start

Players with zero decided games sort last, ordered by the random draw. Raw points scored is not used to rank — it's shown only for context.

Stack (ladder)

Per decided game, a player gains:

multiplier = 1 + 0.5 · (courtCount − court) / (courtCount − 1) gain = points · multiplier + 3 // if the team won + 2 // extra, if the win was below the top court (a climb)

With two courts, the top court's multiplier is 1.5× and the bottom is 1.0×. Players are ranked by average gain per game (stackScore ÷ games), ties broken by the random draw.

King of the Court

Per decided game, points scored accumulate, wins are tallied, and wins on Court 1 (the King's Court) are tallied separately. The score is:

kingScore = wins + pointsScored + kingCourtWins

Players are ranked by average King score per game (kingScore ÷ games), ties broken by the random draw.

Finals seeding

After the regular rounds, the format's final ranking is computed and players are sliced into groups of four — top four to the Championship (seed 1 vs 4, seed 2 vs 3), the next four to Consolation, then Bronze, and so on, one bracket per available court. Anyone below the last complete group of four is listed as unseated. Finals games are excluded from every regular-season statistic, so they never alter the standings that produced the seeding. In Mixed mode, finals pairings also respect 1M+1W per team.

What counts as a "decided" game

Only games with a finished, win-by-2 result feed the statistics. A tied score (e.g. 11–11) is treated as in-progress and excluded from points, wins, and margins until it resolves — so an unfinished game can never pad the standings. Byes are not games and never count.

This page reflects the scoring constants defined in the app. If those are ever tuned, this page is updated to match.
